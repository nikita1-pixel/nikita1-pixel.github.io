📌 The problem

  • Lending Club is a peer-to-peer lender — the entire business rests on predicting who repays and who doesn't.
  • Two questions a credit team actually asks: does the A–G grading really sort risk, and how much money is genuinely exposed — in dollars, not percentages?

🔧 What I did

  • Cleaned 2.26M raw loans → 1.3M analysis-ready rows: fixed text-stored types, triaged missing values by size, dropped junk (e.g. negative debt ratios, placeholder caps).
  • Avoided data leakage — kept only ~22 features knowable at approval time, so findings would hold for real future loans.
  • Built interactive dashboards in Power BI & Tableau, then added an AI insight layer in Python.

📊 What I found

  • The grading system works: default climbs cleanly 6% (A) → 50% (G) — Grade G defaults 8× more often than Grade A.
  • A pricing tension: Grade G defaults 50% of the time but is charged only ~28% interest — is that enough to cover the losses?
  • Loan term matters: 60-month loans default 33% vs 16% for 36-month — double the risk.
  • Why someone borrows predicts repayment: small business 30% (riskiest) vs weddings 12% (safest).
  • The surprise: employment length barely differs between defaulters and repayers — it does not predict default.
  • The dollar view is scarier than the count view: 21.6% of money is at risk vs ~20% of loans — defaulted loans skew larger.

✅ Insights for change

  • Re-price or tighten approval on F/G grades — 28% interest can't cover a 50% default rate.
  • Treat 60-month loans as a separate, higher-risk tier — they default at double the rate.
  • Add loan purpose to risk scoring — small-business loans need extra scrutiny or collateral.
  • Stop weighting employment length as a default signal — the data says it doesn't discriminate.
  • Raise reserves on grades D–G (AI-flagged) — ~$2.25B of exposure sits there.
The difference this makes: the project doesn't stop at "30% of loans default." It pinpoints which loans, quantifies the $4.06B at stake, and hands a credit team specific, testable changes — the jump from describing the past to changing the outcome.
